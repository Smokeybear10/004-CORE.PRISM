A note from the team · April 2026

Most price moves are noise. The ones that aren't, are stories.

PRISM reads the story around a stock move, decomposes it into five measurable dimensions of cause, and decides whether to lean with the move or fade it. We're not chasing alpha. We're asking a sharper question — what did the market actually hear, and is what it heard durable?

I · The thesis

Decompose language, then test whether the decomposition pays.

Markets price information. Information arrives in language — earnings calls, SEC filings, analyst notes, news. The price reaction to that language is what we want to understand. Two questions follow, and they are the only two questions PRISM tries to answer:

What did the market actually hear?
Not the headline. The structural read. Was the move driven by demand growth, by pricing power, by a competitive shift, by management losing credibility, or by a macro shock the company happened to sit in front of? The cause matters more than the magnitude.
Is what it heard durable?
Some causes persist — demand cycles, margin expansion, share gains. Others revert — single-quarter mgmt stumbles, macro fears, sector-wide sympathy moves. The same 10% drop can be a buy or a sell depending on which bucket the cause sits in.
II · The decomposition

Five dimensions. Cited evidence per dimension. No exceptions.

Sentiment flattens reasoning into a single scalar from −1 to +1. PRISM decomposes the same text into five named dimensions, each with a direction, a weight, and at least one citation back to a chunk of source text. Without the citation, the score is dropped. No uncited dimension survives.

Demand
Unit volume, customer count, market share shifts.
Pricing
Price changes, mix, discounting, margin moves.
Competitive
New entrants, competitor moves, moat erosion.
Mgmt credibility
Guidance changes, execution, leadership comments.
Macro
Rates, FX, commodities, geopolitics, sector flows.
III · The pitch in one line

"We're not building a sentiment classifier. We're building a financial historian — one that reads the text around a price move and tells you which cause is doing the work, with the receipts."

— Project brief, Mentor Meeting #1

IV · How we judge ourselves

Ablations, baselines, and a hand-authored ground truth.

A model that scores well on cherry-picked cases isn't a model. Three checks keep us honest:

Ablations
Each event runs through the model with progressively more context — news only, then SEC filings, then earnings transcripts, then peer news, then macro, then institutional positioning. The deltas across runs tell us which sources actually move the verdict. Sources that don't move it don't earn a slot.
Baselines
The verdict has to beat random attribution, always lean, always fade, and a sentiment-only classifier on the same text. Anything less and we're dressing up sentiment in a longer prompt.
Ground truth
Six hand-authored cases — moves the team understands well enough to write the expected verdict before running the model. The harness reports X / N. Today we're at 4 / 6. The two misses are both Nvidia-sympathy patterns the model insists are fundamental — a useful failure to point at, not paper over.

VI · What we won't claim

Honest about the failure modes.

Foreknowledge
The model was trained on financial journalism that post-dates many events. We can't fully strip that out; we acknowledge it and lean on the ablation deltas — those are within-model and compare cleanly.
Survivorship
Yahoo's panel doesn't carry delisted firms. Our universe is biased toward survivors. Reported hit rates are upper bounds.
Coverage gaps
The bundled news parquet starts in early 2025; older moves rely on filings and transcripts. The toggles in the dashboard make this explicit per event.
One-shot prompts
No reflection, no debate, no multi-turn reasoning. The model gets one chance per move. Improvements there are obvious next steps, not hidden tricks.
VII · The story

Tagging is the product. The trade is the stress test.

The deliverable is not a trading strategy. It's the decomposition, the citations, and the framework for testing whether the decomposition pays. If a desk used PRISM the morning after a flagged move, the question they should be asking is not what's the verdict? — it's which cause is doing the work, what's the citation, and is that the kind of cause that lasts?

Click Dashboard and try it on AMD's 2025-04-09 rally. The model reads it as fundamental. The team's hand-authored answer says it's Nvidia-sympathy and Trump-tariff news, not real demand. The disagreement is the kind of thing this project exists to surface.
